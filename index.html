The 5 Principles of the Mental Capacity Act

Before you begin, remember the five statutory principles that are the foundation of the Mental Capacity Act 2005. They are designed to protect and empower individuals who may lack capacity.

1. Assume Capacity: Every adult has the right to make their own decisions and must be assumed to have capacity to do so unless it is proved otherwise.
2. Provide Support: A person must be given all practicable help before anyone treats them as not being able to make their own decisions.
3. Unwise Decisions: A person is not to be treated as unable to make a decision merely because they make an unwise decision.
4. Best Interests: An act done, or decision made, under this Act for or on behalf of a person who lacks capacity must be done, or made, in their best interests.
5. Least Restrictive: Before the act is done, or the decision is made, regard must be had to whether the purpose for which it is needed can be as effectively achieved in a way that is less restrictive of the personâ€™s rights and freedom of action.

Important Consideration: Coercive Control

Coercive control can significantly impact a person's ability to make a decision, even if they appear to have capacity. The tactics used by perpetrators (e.g., intimidation, isolation, threats) can limit a person's ability to make independent decisions.

Implications for practice:

  • Be aware that the person may be adapting their decisions to minimise risk of harm.
  • Remember that the person knows their situation best. Do not impose or force a decision; focus on building trust.
  • Your professional skills are crucial in creating a safe space for the person to make their own decisions.
  • Always be clear about any indicators of coercive control when raising a concern with the local authority.
